Somebody Opened a Door

Now we are holding it open for others.

The Summer of 1983

People often ask why Accoladi exists.

The answer goes back to the summer of 1983.

I was 23 years old and had just started my first teaching job as band director at a small rural high school in coastal South Carolina. On my third day, I learned it was Senior Day. Twelve seniors were gathering for their yearbook pictures, and each student was asked to describe their future.

The dreams they shared were simple.

One young man wanted a pickup truck.

Three young women wanted to see the ocean for the first time.

Another student wanted to afford McDonald's every Friday night.

What struck me most was not what they said. It was what they did not say: not one of them mentioned college.

The Question

So I asked a question.

"Where are you planning to go to college?"

The room got very quiet.

Not one of the twelve students planned to attend college. Most had never seriously considered it an option.

I was stunned.

The truth is, I had grown up with music teachers who gave me something that changed my life: a year-by-year plan showing how music could become a pathway to college admission, scholarships, and opportunity.

I simply assumed every student had received that same guidance.

These twelve students had not.

The Trip

That afternoon, I canceled marching band rehearsal.

Instead, I loaded the seniors into a school van and we took a little trip.

First, we stopped at a Chevrolet dealership. The young man who wanted a pickup truck learned that his dream truck was not impossible. With steady work and manageable payments, it could become real.

Next, we went to McDonald's.

Ten of the twelve students had never eaten there before.

I paid for everyone's meal and told them to supersize their orders.

What many people would call an ordinary lunch became something memorable. For these students, it was not about hamburgers or fries. It was about realizing there were experiences, opportunities, and possibilities beyond what they had imagined for themselves.

Then we drove to the beach.

The Promise

I will never forget watching those three young ladies see the Atlantic Ocean for the first time.

By the end of the day, we found ourselves sitting at a Krispy Kreme counter.

And that's where I made them a promise.

"If you'll work, I'll help you earn a college scholarship."

Over the next several months, we got to work.

They learned how to prepare auditions.

They learned how to complete college applications.

They learned how to apply for scholarships and financial aid.

Most importantly, they began to believe that college was actually possible.

September and October were spent completing applications. January and February were spent traveling to auditions in my 1974 Chevrolet Maverick.

The Auditions

In just seven weeks, those twelve students auditioned at seventeen colleges and universities in seven different states.

For many of them, it was the first time they had ever stepped onto a college campus.

Every one of them was accepted.

Every one of them received scholarships, grants, or financial assistance.

And every one of them went on to earn a college degree.

What Changed

Today, those students are in their sixties. They have served as principals, school administrators, teachers, military musicians, church musicians, and band directors.

They raised families, built careers, and made meaningful contributions to their communities.

But their story changed me, too.

It taught me something I have never forgotten.

Why Accoladi Exists Today

Throughout my career as a music educator, and later as a college dean, I kept meeting talented students who did not lack ability. They lacked information, guidance, and a plan.

That realization stayed with me for decades.

Eventually, it became the foundation for Accoladi.

Today, Accoladi exists for the same reason I loaded twelve seniors into a school van in 1983: to help students and families understand that talent, when combined with preparation and opportunity, can change a life.

Every student deserves what my teachers gave me.

Every student deserves what those twelve seniors discovered.

A plan. A mentor. And someone who believes their dreams are possible.

That Promise Continues Today.

With gratitude for the teachers who believed in me, the students who inspired me, and the performing arts that changed all of our lives.

— Dr. Randall Bayne
Founder, Accoladi
